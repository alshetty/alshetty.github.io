General Information:

Known to the scientific community as Cucurbita, the squash is a genus of herbaceous, or non-woody, vines. These gourds are grown worldwide for both their fruit and their seeds. Most species’ vines grow several meters in length and produce yellow and orange flowers before bearing fruit. While all squashes are rich in nutrients, most notably vitamin A and vitamin C, the vegetables tend to be split up into two categories: winter squash and summer squash.

There are a few key differences between these two varieties. The full grown fruits of winter squash can be stored for months. Their seeds are fully matured and their skin has fully hardened into a tough rind. Winter squash is generally cooked before being eaten, but the rind is usually discarded. Common examples of winter squash include pumpkins, butternut squash, and acorn squash. On the other hand, summer squash is harvested when still immature and the rind is still tender and edible. Their vines don’t ramble as much as winter squashes, and are instead more bushy in nature. Some common summer squashes are zucchini, patty pan squashes, and straightneck squash.
